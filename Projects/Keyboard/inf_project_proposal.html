3.1 Summary

This project will design a fast input device for the present and forthcoming age of human computer interaction (mobile -> XR) primerally looking to build a viable alternative typewriter style keyboard. Specifically looking to design, build and develop software around a portable handheld chorded input device (keyboard).

3.2 Why? Bandwidth: The master variable

Where does the human end and the computer begin? The answer appears to be that it doesn’t(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_mind_thesis#%22The_Extended_Mind%22). We become ever more entwined with our technology; one day it becomes inseparable and indistinguishable from us. Many argue this is already so. Perhaps then a better question is how this merger happens?

Neurotechnology company Neuralink (https://neuralink.com) is a frontrunner building ambitious implantable brain-machine interfaces to reach this goal. Founder Elon Musk can regularly be heard framing their fundamental mission in terms of “bandwidth”, proposing that the current input/output channels between human and computer are relatively slow and limited, and a scale factor increase is the key to advancing human-computer symbiosis.

I also believe that bandwidth is the master variable. Consider what would happen if you were able navigate a computer with comparable speed, ease and conceptual abstraction to your internal thoughts.

Hence for this project I have chosen to target human-computer bandwidth.

> Goal 1: Increase human-computer bandwidth by a scale factor

> Subgoal 1.1: Research and develop a more robust and quantifiable concept of human-computer bandwidth.

3.3 What? A bandwidth chokepoint: typewriter keyboards

Analysis of the current human-computer input/output landscape lead me to identifying the (typewriter) keyboard as a bandwidth chokepoint for the following reasons:

  1. Input is a bigger bottleneck than output. (LCD displays and VR headsets can produce more visual stimulus than you can process, immersing the totality of your senses)
  2. Keyboards (commands) are more conceptually complex than mice (point and inspect), making more room for improvement (although if possible functionality for both would be ideal)
  3. Since the typewriters conception (~1710) the design hasn’t changed much in its evolution to modern standard keyboard. It might be possible to capitalise on design decisions made for contingent reasons which are antithetical to modern design needs.
  4. Keyboards are obviously un-ergonomic with respect to the hands that operate them
    • The 2D grid layout is not natural for hand movements
    • Keyboards cannot be easily operated without looking
  5. Keyboards seem hard to learn, despite speeds of 100+ wpm being possible, the average American can only type at 30wpm despite the fact that a majority of them use one everyday.
  6. Keyboards are not naturally suited to mobile (moving) useage, (their mobile touchscreen variants are even slower and less ergonomic)
    • you need to be static to use one, even your hands must remain in a single contorted position
  7. Keyboards are not suitable for VR/AR, which many think is the future (https://www.macrumors.com/roundup/apple-glasses/, https://tech.fb.com/ar-vr/)
    • there is no known solution for mobile text input in VR (beyond point and click on a virtual keyboard).
  8. Keyboards and mice cannot be used simultaneously. This is a massive bandwidth bottleneck, you have to reposition your hands twice.

I conclude that a developing portable wireless handheld ergonomic input device might have a chance of solving all of these probelms. Beyond a minimum working product I will focusing on design decisions that increase bandwidth (faster input, higher level abstraction, power user features). I will be using typing-speed in wpm as good approximation for the success of this project.

> Goal 0: Build an portable handheld input device (keyboard) capable of 300wpm

I don’t really know how ambitious this goal is. I took the idea vaguely from Peter Theil who say that for an innovation to matter it has to be 10x better than the alternative. 30wpm (the average) x 10 = 300wpm.

3.4 How? Academic scope and technical aspects of the project

3.4.1 Physical Design

This will include designing a casing (CAD), printing it and sourcing key switches.

3.4.2 Electrical Design

Selecting and fitting an appropriate microcontroller and soldering the design together

3.4.3 Keymap Design

This will be the main theoretical area of the project. Fundamentally there will be some mapping between key-switch-presses and output actions (print character, backspace, function call, etc. )

An in depth review of theory and implementations of keymaps will be conducted in the service of designing a better one (that maximises input bandwidth). It’s hard to know exactly what will be most relevent at this stage but I intend to research concepts such as chorded keyboards, signal compression and linguistic symbol systems.

Similarly, possibilities to extend (and reduce) the classical keymap will be considered. Is the current human-computer interaction functional paradigm optimal? Is there functionality that could be moved into (or out of) the input device, unifying commands across interfaces. For example, how are unicode characters entered? Could mouse input be unified?

> Goal 2: Research the emergent language of human computer interaction. What are the fundamental operations? How can we build this into a universal input device.

This logic with then have to be programmed into the keyboard in a way that is well documented and extensible to others who wish to use the product.

3.4.4 Software

All further features will be software related. This will could include things such as an inbuilt abbreviations dictionary, and tutorial, autosuggestions, a standalone UI, etc. I envision that many of the innovations needed to increase bandwidth will be software.
